Chapter one · Memory

The weight of small things

Every room is a private museum. A chipped cup becomes a calendar; a theatre stub, a doorway. We arrange these quiet witnesses around us until their stories become inseparable from our own.

Memory does not live in the object itself, but in the distance between hand and surface. The polished edge of a table keeps an account of every passing body. Linen retains the shape of sleep. Silver darkens faithfully in the air.

These are not monuments. They ask for no audience. Their power is precisely their scale: the humble evidence of having been here.

Chapter three · Patina

A beautiful undoing

We call it damage when time becomes visible. Yet patina is nothing more than duration made legible: rain working on stone, oil warming timber, fingers burnishing brass.

The contemporary world is taught to resist such evidence. Surfaces arrive sealed, flawless, mute. But perfection has no biography. It offers the eye nowhere to rest and the imagination nothing to continue.

To cherish wear is to accept that beauty may be accumulative — that a thing can become more fully itself by being used, altered and eventually released.
